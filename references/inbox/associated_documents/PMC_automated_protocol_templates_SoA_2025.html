Abstract

Background

The writing of clinical trial protocols that conform to relevant guidelines is a labor- and time-intensive endeavor. An automated clinical trial protocol template generation system for healthy volunteer trials and a web-based schedule of activities (SoA) generator were developed in this study.

Materials and methods

R Markdown was applied as the primary template development tool to automate the creation of a clinical trial protocol template that adheres to the International Council for Harmonization (ICH) M11 guidelines by enabling the adjustment of dynamic variables and facilitating the standardized presentation of relevant data using associated R packages. To ensure broader compatibility, the template was also tested using Quarto, which produced identical outputs with slight modifications to the YAML header. Additionally, a web-based SoA generator (developed with React.js) was created, featuring user-friendly functions for flexible start day adjustments, trial parameter settings, and automatic annotations.

Results

The R Markdown script removed most manual effort in creating healthy volunteer protocol templates, thereby increasing both accuracy and productivity. The system also supports Quarto with minor modifications, providing an alternative for users preferring its framework. The web-based SoA generator also demonstrated significant advantages over conventional methods, as it reduced human errors and required less time compared to manually generating SoAs in Word documents.

Conclusions

This study introduces a novel automated protocol template generation process using R Markdown as the primary tool, with Quarto compatibility confirmed, and a web-based SoA generator designed specifically for healthy volunteer trials. By improving the efficiency and accuracy of clinical trial document preparation, these tools have the potential to advance the drug development process.

1. Introduction

In the drug development process, the clinical stage involves the generation of crucial documents, such as clinical trial protocols, informed consent forms, investigator brochures, investigational new drug annual reports, and clinical study reports [1]. These documents are essential for maintaining regulatory standards, ethical practices, and scientific rigor and thus play a pivotal role in the integrity and success of clinical trials. Among these, the clinical trial protocol is particularly critical as it outlines the trial's objectives, design, methodology, statistical considerations, and logistical details, marking the first step toward conducting a high-quality trial [2,3].

The traditional creation of clinical trial protocols is a manual, time-intensive process prone to human error and inefficiencies [4], especially when adhering to guidelines, such as those of the International Council for Harmonization Good Clinical Practice (ICH-GCP). The Standard Protocol Items: Recommendations for Interventional Trials (SPIRIT) Statement and similar guidelines recommend specific items for inclusion to ensure comprehensive and clear protocols. However, manual documentation of adherence adds complexity and the potential for errors [5], particularly in creating schedule of activities (SoA) tables for protocols. These tables are essential but are time-consuming and susceptible to human error, leading to inefficiencies and inconsistencies in trial execution and management.

Over the past few decades, there have been several attempts to automate aspects of clinical trial protocol generation. Existing tools like Protocol Builder™ by BRANY [6] and e-Protocol by National Institutes of Health (NIH) [7] were designed to standardize protocol structures, ensuring regulatory compliance and document consistency. However, while these tools support some degree of standardization, they often fall short in addressing the more specific demands of early-phase trials involving healthy volunteers. Particularly, they require significant manual input for trial-specific customization, making them less efficient for protocols that involve repetitive or standardized procedures, such as pharmacokinetic sampling procedures or frequent safety assessments common in healthy volunteer studies.

In response to the limitations of current systems, we were to develop a novel approach to automate early-phase, healthy volunteer-involved trial protocols using R Markdown and React.js. R Markdown served as the foundation for this system, providing an easy way to write a markdown document [8] and that enabling structured automation, dynamic document generation, and reproducibility. The system is also compatible with Quarto, which is the next generation version of R Markdown [9]. The template requires only minimal modifications, such as minor adjustments to the YAML header to demonstrate the same output between.rmd and.qmd. This interchangeability allows researchers to choose between R Markdown and Quarto based on their preferences while maintaining the same core functionalities. These technologies offer significant advantages for managing complexity and frequent updates required in such trials. R Markdown and Quarto enables consistent integration of text, code, and outputs in a single document [10], ensuring that changes in parameters—such as drug names, dosages, or visit schedules—are automatically reflected throughout the protocol. This minimizes manual editing and enhances both consistency and traceability, which are critical for regulatory compliance. React.js complements this by providing real-time interactivity, allowing investigators to dynamically adjust elements in the SoA in a web-based interface, a functionality often lacking in the previous protocol automation systems. This capability is particularly valuable, as SoAs frequently require updates during protocol development.

This study aimed to demonstrate the feasibility of developing an open-source software tool for protocol generation that can be applied to early-phase healthy volunteer trials by utilizing the aforementioned features. By detailing both the source code developed during the process and the functionalities derived from it, it is expected to establish the foundational reliability of the developed system and lay the academic groundwork for the application of such a system to actual protocol generation tasks, including the SoA. Based on the foundational information presented in this article, the authors anticipate that the system will be adopted in practice by sponsors and investigators involved in early-phase healthy volunteer trials. Furthermore, the authors expect that a track record and ongoing functional improvements will be continually accumulated within an open-source environment through active exchanges of feedback from users.

2. Materials and methods

This study introduces an advanced approach for streamlining Phase I clinical trial protocol development, utilizing R Markdown and Quarto for protocol template generation (https://github.com/kateeeeshin/protocol-automation.git) and JavaScript for SoA generation (https://pipet-clinicaltrial-tools.netlify.app/).

2.1. Clinical protocol template generation with R Markdown and Quarto

A clinical protocol template was primarily generated using R Markdown, adhering to the ICH M11 guidelines and requiring minimal human interference during the documentation process. A structured template was established and the necessary variables and parameters were identified across various protocol sections. The template leverages R Markdown's YAML header for metadata configuration, and a cover page for the complex protocol was obtained in the officedown:rdocx_document output format. The script also utilized the R Markdown syntax for headings (# for main headings, ## for subheadings, etc.) to organize the document into coherent sections, adhering to the ICH M11 guidelines. The body content was generated using plain text within the R Markdown script to ensure a straightforward narrative flow. This approach ensures the incorporation of pre-defined word document styles, thus facilitating a seamless and consistent output. Additionally, we incorporated the block_toc(.) function to automatically generate a Table of Contents (TOC) for the protocol document. This feature dynamically creates a TOC based on the sections and subsections defined in the R Markdown document, thereby enhancing navigability and accessibility. Moreover, to test compatibility with Quarto, the same script was executed with a modified YAML header, ensuring that the document structure, formatting, and content remained consistent across both formats. While R Markdown remains the primary development platform, this validation confirmed that users have the flexibility to utilize Quarto if preferred.

2.2. Dynamic variable replacement

Although Phase I trial designs can vary slightly, healthy volunteer protocols typically contain many repetitive elements. By accurately setting up dynamic variables—such as drug names, protocol numbers, and dosage information—the system ensures consistent and accurate auto-updating of these details throughout the document. The knitrknit_code$get(.) and set(.) functions within the “knitr” package and str_replace_all(.) in the “stringr” package were used to allow for automatic updating of these elements within the code chunks across the document, minimizing manual adjustments and errors. This system ensures that updates to the protocol number, drug names, total subject number, and sponsor name are reflected throughout the document upon rendering.

2.3. Special format generation

The “flextable” package in R was used for generating tables and figures. This approach guarantees uniformity in table presentations aligned with the protocol's professional standards. Specifically, tables were generated to present detailed drug information and definitions of adverse events, ensuring a clean and standardized format. Figures and tables are seamlessly integrated into the document with a focus on maintaining readability and coherence, thus facilitating a comprehensive understanding of the intricacies of the protocol.

2.4. Abbreviation extraction

The system implemented an automated abbreviation extraction feature to facilitate the identification and compilation of commonly used abbreviations within the protocol document. This process was executed using the “stringr” and “flextable” packages in R.

To identify abbreviations, the system employed str_match_all(.), which applied a regular expression pattern to detect sequences of two or more uppercase letters enclosed in parentheses. The extracted abbreviations were stored as a dataset with duplicates removed and entries sorted alphabetically. The extracted abbreviations were subsequently formatted into a structured glossary table using “flextable”. While the system automated abbreviation detection and table formatting, definitions for the abbreviations remained manually input by users before finalizing the glossary. The compiled glossary table was then integrated into the final protocol document as appendix.

2.5. Web-based SoA generator interface

React.js was used to build a dynamic and responsive user interface (UI) for the SoA generator, enabling users to input and modify trial activity parameters, such as start dates, periods, and key trial milestones. Functions for data saving and retrieval were implemented using “localStorage”, allowing users to save and access SoAs. Additionally, a series of interactive forms and checkboxes were implemented for users to select trial parameters and automatically generate annotations in accordance with the user input parameters within the SoA. To maintain the input state of the generated table, the “Recoil” package was used. “Recoil.js” was employed to store the input state of the table, which serves as the basis for calculating data, such as PERIOD, WASHOUT, OPD (out-patient department), and PSV (post-study visit), based on the added parameters. Subsequently, the calculated values were stored in “Recoil.js” for future reference. Once the table is generated, annotations are created, where required. Upon selecting checkboxes in the table generated by users, the input state of the table (maintained by Recoil) is altered. React.js “useEffect” hook changes in the input state of the table could be detected, triggering a function to update the annotations.

2.6. Test of clinical trial protocol generation

A comparative evaluation was conducted using an actual clinical trial protocol to validate the accuracy and functionality of the automated clinical trial protocol generation system. A completed trial registered on ClinicalTrials.gov (ID: NCT04406194) [11] was selected for this test run as a representative example of a typical healthy volunteer study with a 2 × 2 crossover bioequivalence design, which is one of the most commonly used methodologies in such trials. The selection of the trial for test run was made considering key trial characteristics, including study objective, design, schedule, and etc. The evaluation process consisted of three key assessment stages: system integration and data extraction, completeness/compliance check, and accuracy/consistency assessment.

3. Results

3.1. Implementation

3.1.1. The system setup

The automated protocol generation system is available as an open-source project using R Markdown or Quarto, which are accessible via Integrated Development Environment (IDE) such as RStudio [12]. It is designed to ensure broad compatibility as long as the system supports R Markdown or Quarto execution.

To set up the system, users must first install the required dependencies. The necessary R packages are listed in the GitHub repository's README.md file and should be installed accordingly. Package installation can be done within R using the install.packages(.) function. After installing dependencies, users need to clone the GitHub repository to obtain the source files. This can be done using the following command in a terminal or command prompt:

git clone https://github.com/kateeeeshin/protocol-automation.git.

Once the repository is cloned, users must download and ensure that the reference Word template (word_template.docx) is placed in the correct directory. The script is configured to use a relative path (“./word-template.docx”), meaning that the template file must be one level above the directory where Phase1_2X2_Healthy_Template.Rmd is located. If the file is placed in a different folder, users need to update the path within the YAML metadata of the R Markdown script to match the correct location. If the template file is missing or incorrectly placed, the system will not render properly and an error message regarding the reference document will be displayed.

3.1.2. The workflow

After setting up the system and ensuring all dependencies are installed, users can generate a clinical trial protocol by executing the Phase1_2X2_Healthy_Template.Rmd file in RStudio. The process follows a structured workflow, where users input study-specific details, execute the script by ‘knit’ button, obtain a formatted protocol document, generate SoA table using SoA generator, and combine it with formatted protocol to get complete protocol. The complete workflow of generating a protocol document using two systems is illustrated in Fig. 1.

3.2.6. Web-based SoA generator interface

The tool successfully automated the creation of a standardized SoA table, ensuring proper alignment and organization of study activities and visit schedules. However, differences were observed when compared to the original NCT04406194 protocol SoA due to variations in day counting methodology and trial-specific parameters that are not yet integrated into the current version of the system.

A key distinction between the system-generated SoA and the original schedule is the approach to day counting. In the original protocol, washout periods are not included in the numerical sequence of study days, whereas the SoA generator automatically calculates and assigns days based on actual elapsed time. Additionally, for the post-study visit (PSV, described as ‘Final Examination’ in the original protocol), the original protocol combines the discharge day and PSV on the same day, which the system was unable to represent correctly. Since the SoA generator currently does not support overlapping visit days, PSV was omitted from the main table. Instead, it was manually included in the annotation section, ensuring that all required assessments were still documented. Moreover, trial-specific assessments, such as COVID-19 testing, were not automatically included in the generated table because current of the SoA generator does not yet support user-defined trial-specific parameters. However, these assessments were manually added as annotations under Laboratory Tests allowing for accurate representation of planned study procedures.

The SoA table was generated and integrated into the final protocol through the following steps (Supplementary Material 1).

  • 1)

    Authors entered key study parameters such as start day, periods, and washout into the SoA generator.

  • 2)

    The system formatted the basic table, automatically calculating day sequences and structuring visit schedules as authors input parameters.

  • 3)

    Authors mapped out checkboxes in each study activity according to the NCT04406194 trial schedule.

  • 4)

    Annotations were automatically created based on the input parameters in the table, and then authors adjusted them to reflect trial-specific details that could not be directly integrated into the table.

  • 5)

    The SoA table was exported as an image using ‘EXPORT’ function and embedded into the final Word document.

  • 6)

    The generated annotations were copied and pasted to the final Word document using ‘COPY’ function in SoA table.

The system demonstrated a substantial reduction in time and effort required to generate a clinical trial protocol for NCT04406194 [11], improving standardization and minimizing human errors. The R Markdown & Quarto-based system effectively integrated predefined study parameters, automated section formatting, and ensured compliance with standard protocol structures. While section population and document formatting were fully automated, certain sections like Background, Schema, Inclusion/Exclusion Criteria, required additional manual input to align with the original protocol. The system successfully adhered to ICH M11 requirements but completeness was partial due to differences in how trial-specific procedures were incorporated.

For the SoA Generator, automated scheduling provided a structured visit table, but deviations from NCT04406194's original day count approach were observed. The system accurately calculated elapsed days, including washout periods, whereas the original protocol excluded washout periods from sequential day numbering. The system also could not represent Post-Study Visit (PSV) on the same day as discharge, requiring its inclusion in the annotation section instead. Similarly, COVID-19 testing and other trial-specific assessments were not automatically included but were manually added in annotations to ensure protocol completeness.

Despite few differences, the system successfully formatted essential study components ensuring that all required parameters were documented. The total time required to generate the first draft of the protocol using the systems was 52 min and 44 s which significantly reduced the manual workload compared to conventional protocol drafting methods (taking at least six weeks for a complete simple early-phase study protocol document [13]).

4. Discussion

This study confirms that an automated protocol generation tool can be successfully developed in an open-source environment, primarily developed using R Markdown, with validated compatibility for Quarto. The strength of this tool is most evident in early-stage clinical trials, especially those involving healthy volunteers. These trials are ideal candidates for automation because they are highly standardized and repetitive: variables such as drug names or sample sizes may vary, but the overall protocol framework remains largely consistent. R Markdown and Quarto's dynamic variable management feature ensures that changes to key variables are automatically reflected throughout the protocol, maintaining consistency without the need for manual editing. This consistency is critical in early clinical trials, where accuracy and consistency across documents is essential to ensure compliance and reduce the risk of errors.

One of the main challenges in early-stage protocol writing is the creation of the SoA, which is the most labor-intensive and error-prone aspect of protocol development. Existing tools often rely on static HTML table templates, which require manual input from users, significantly increasing the effort involved. However, by integrating React.js, the system can automate this process, allowing for real-time updates, automatic annotations, and the formatting of tables based on predefined criteria. This approach not only reduces the time required for revisions, but also ensures consistency across documents, improving protocol accuracy and lowering the likelihood of human error.

The escalating costs of drug development underscore the need for innovative strategies that streamline study protocols and eliminate unnecessary repetition. Recent commentary has noted that such approaches can lead to faster, more cost-effective clinical trials [14]. In this context, automation plays a crucial role by simplifying and standardizing early-stage clinical trials that typically involve healthy volunteers, adhere to standardized designs, and operate on tight timelines [15]. By reducing protocol development time, automation not only enhances productivity in these fast-paced studies but also provides the flexibility needed to schedule subsequent trials more effectively. This efficiency aligns with the broader “kill fast” concept in drug development, which emphasizes the early identification and elimination of unsuccessful candidates to conserve resources [14]. Moreover, since early-stage trial budgets are typically smaller [16], automation becomes a valuable tool for reducing labor costs without sacrificing accuracy or compliance. Overall, adopting automation tools for protocol generation and management of SoA is an important factor in increasing the efficiency and value of early-phase clinical trials in healthy volunteers.

Despite the promising advances presented in this study, important limitations must be acknowledged. One of the primary limitations is the lack of comprehensive performance evaluation in real-world clinical settings. While the system demonstrated efficiency in generating protocol templates and SoA tables, empirical data on its actual impact—such as time reduction, error minimization, and user experience feedbacks—remains insufficient. Future studies will focus on addressing these limitations by collecting empirical data during real clinical trials and exploring potential regulatory considerations. Another limitation is that the current system has been primarily designed for early-phase, healthy volunteer trials and may require further modifications to accommodate more complex clinical trial designs. Adaptive trials, multi-arm studies, and platform trials involve dynamically changing parameters, interim analyses, and more intricate randomization strategies, which are not yet supported. For instance, in adaptive trials, decision-making during interim analyses influences subsequent study procedures requiring a dynamic and iterative update mechanism that the current system lacks. Similarly, platform trials necessitate real-time modifications to trial arms based on accumulating data which would require enhanced interoperability with electronic data capture (EDC) and randomization management systems like randomization and trial supply management (RTSM). Expanding the system's flexibility to integrate with such methodologies will be crucial for its broader applicability.

Furthermore, while this research has demonstrated that the system is compatible with Quarto, its full potential for extending functionality has yet to be explored. Quarto's flexibility in supporting multiple programming languages (R, Python, Julia) suggests that additional computational capabilities [9], such as advanced statistical modeling or machine learning-driven protocol optimization, could be integrated in future iterations. Moreover, Quarto's adaptability to API-based interactions makes it a strong candidate for enhancing interoperability with digital clinical trial systems like eConsent, EDC, or clinical trial management systems (CTMS). Quarto's ability to generate dynamic documents could also be applied to automate writing clinical study report. By integrating with CTMS and EDC platforms, the system could automate the creation of study reports, safety monitoring summaries, and interim analysis reports using real-time data from trial databases. Automating this process would help ensure that trial stakeholders receive timely updates, reducing administrative workload and improving oversight efficiency. Future research should explore how Quarto's flexibility in handling multiple programming languages and its API-driven automation can be utilized to enhance clinical trial documentation and reporting workflows.

5. Conclusion

This study demonstrates the potential of automation to improve protocol development for early-phase clinical trials with healthy volunteers. The integration of R Markdown/Quarto and React.js enabled automated generation of protocol templates and SoA tables, reducing manual input, minimizing errors, and ensuring consistency throughout the document. Although further customization and validation in large-scale, real-world settings are required, the results presented here establish a foundation for more efficient and cost-effective protocol development. Future work will concentrate on refining system functionality, expanding customization options, and collecting empirical data to assess scalability and practical application across a broader range of clinical trials.
